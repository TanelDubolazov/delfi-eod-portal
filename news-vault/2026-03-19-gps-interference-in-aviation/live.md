For the third consecutive day, signal jamming has made landing under difficult conditions hazardous. The Defence Forces are monitoring the situation; a hybrid attack from a neighboring country is suspected.

### Crisis at Tartu Airport

The ongoing GPS interference has severely impacted operations at **Tartu Airport (TAY)**, a critical regional hub. Unlike larger international airports, Tartu relies heavily on GPS-based approach procedures, making it exceptionally vulnerable to signal disruption. For the third day, the **Finnair** flight from Helsinki, the only regular international connection, was forced to divert back to Finland after the cockpit crew reported a complete loss of reliable satellite navigation data during descent.

"The safety of our passengers is non-negotiable," a Finnair spokesperson stated. "Until stable navigation signals can be guaranteed, approaches to Tartu remain suspended." The cancellations have left dozens of travelers stranded, highlighting the fragility of regional connectivity in the face of modern, electronic warfare tactics. The grounding is a significant blow to local businesses and the university community, which depend on the daily connection.

---

### Anatomy of Interference

Aviation experts explain that the jamming affects the **Global Navigation Satellite System (GNSS)** signals, which include GPS and the European Galileo system. When blocked, aircraft systems lose precision, rendering automated landing procedures impossible during low visibility or difficult weather. Pilots are forced to revert to older, ground-based radio navigation aids, but many regional airfields, like Tartu, lack the comprehensive infrastructure of larger hubs.

The Estonian Transport Administration has issued a **NOTAM** (Notice to Airmen) warning of unreliable GPS signals across the entire eastern Baltic region. The interference is characterized not just by a "blackout," but by a sophisticated, variable spoofing that provides misleading location data, making it even more dangerous.

### Electronic Warfare Monitoring

The Estonian Defence Forces are treating the incident as a coordinated, high-intensity hybrid attack originating from a neighboring country to the east. A specialized unit has deployed mobile electronic intelligence (ELINT) systems to the border region to monitor and triangulate the source of the signals. The equipment, mounted on tactical vehicles, is designed to analyze the specific frequencies and characteristics of the disruptive electronic emissions.

"This is not a technical glitch; this is intentional, aggressive electronic warfare," said Colonel **Andres Lumi** of the Estonian Air Force. The ELINT units are tracking multiple high-powered transmitters, consistent with advanced Russian electronic warfare systems. While the monitoring is defensive, the data collected is critical for international diplomatic efforts and potential countermeasures.

The situation remains dynamic and tense, with no immediate resolution in sight. Estonia is coordinating its response with NATO allies and international aviation authorities, as the interference is increasingly being viewed as a threat to regional stability and civilian air safety. Until a diplomatic or technical solution is found, air travel to regional hubs like Tartu will likely face continued disruption.
