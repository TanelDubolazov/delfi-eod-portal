Crude oil supply difficulties and new excise duty changes are hitting the transport sector hard. Logistics firms warn of significant price hikes for services.

### Market Under Unprecedented Pressure

The Estonian fuel market is bracing for a historic shock as retail experts warn that diesel prices at the pump could reach the **€3.00 per liter** mark by the end of the week. This potential surge is driven by a "double-tap" of economic factors: a sudden tightening of global crude oil supply routes and the early implementation of the government’s revised excise duty package.

Major retailers, including Olerex and Circle K, have noted that wholesale acquisition costs have become increasingly volatile over the last 72 hours. "We are seeing a market that has decoupled from traditional seasonal trends," says market analyst **Mari Maasikas**. "The supply chain is being squeezed at the refinery level, and these costs are inevitably flowing down to the consumer."

### Logistics Sector on High Alert

The transport and logistics industry, which forms the backbone of the Estonian economy, is already feeling the strain. Several prominent trucking firms have issued warnings that current contracts may need to be renegotiated to include emergency "fuel surcharges." For many, the €3 threshold represents a psychological and financial breaking point.

If prices remain at this elevated level, the cost of transporting food, construction materials, and consumer goods will rise sharply. The **Association of Estonian International Road Carriers** has called for an emergency meeting with the Ministry of Economic Affairs to discuss a temporary freeze on the excise duty increase to prevent a total "logistical standstill."

### Consumer Impact and Future Outlook

For the average resident, the price hike at the pump is only the first wave of the impact. Economists predict that a sustained diesel price above €2.50 will lead to a broader inflationary spike across all retail sectors. Public transport operators in Tallinn and Tartu are also reviewing their budgets, as the cost of maintaining bus fleets continues to climb.

As long as the supply difficulties persist in the Baltic region, fuel stations are likely to see frequent price adjustments. Motorists are being advised to plan their trips more efficiently and monitor price apps closely. While the government has yet to announce any formal intervention, the pressure is mounting to provide relief to a sector that literally keeps the country moving.
