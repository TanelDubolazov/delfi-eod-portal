A nationwide failure of bank links and card payments has lasted five hours. Long queues have formed at the few functioning ATMs.

### Digital Payment Gridlock

Estonia’s reputation as a world leader in digital payments faced a severe test today as a massive synchronization error between major clearing banks and the interbank communication provider, **Nets Estonia**, resulted in a total shutdown of card services. From small convenience stores to the largest supermarkets in **Tallinn** and **Narva**, the message on every terminal was the same: "Connection Timeout."

The outage, which began shortly after 1:00 PM, has effectively frozen retail commerce across the country. Since the vast majority of Estonians rely exclusively on contactless and mobile payments, thousands of shoppers were forced to leave full carts at checkout counters. "It’s like the digital heart of the country just stopped beating," said one frustrated customer at a Selver supermarket.

### The Rush for Physical Cash

As news of the card payment failure spread, a secondary crisis emerged at the nation’s ATM network. While many ATMs are tied to the same disrupted communication lines, a limited number of "offline-capable" machines remained operational. This led to the rapid formation of long, anxious queues stretching around city blocks.

By late afternoon, many of these functioning ATMs began running out of banknotes due to the unprecedented withdrawal volume. Banks have advised customers against trying their cards multiple times in disrupted machines, as some terminals have begun "eating" cards after repeated failed authorization attempts. Security firms have been dispatched to the most crowded locations to manage the flow of people and ensure the safety of the remaining cash supplies.

### Investigation into Root Cause

The **Information System Authority (RIA)** is currently working with bank IT teams to determine whether the failure was caused by a routine software update gone wrong or a more malicious external interference. While there is no official confirmation of a cyberattack, the timing of the outage alongside other regional infrastructure issues has raised concerns at the highest levels of government.

"We are treating this as a critical infrastructure failure," a spokesperson for the Ministry of Finance stated. "Our primary goal is to restore the data handshake between the banks and the retail terminals." Until the "green light" is given, residents are advised to carry what little physical cash they have and avoid making large purchases. The banking sector expects a gradual restoration of services by midnight, but the backlog of unverified transactions may cause further "ghost charges" or delays in the coming days.
